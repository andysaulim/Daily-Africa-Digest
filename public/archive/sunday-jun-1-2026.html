Morning Memo
1
The Ebola outbreak in eastern DRC has outpaced the response, drawing WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus to Bunia on May 30. MSF describes the spread as 'deeply alarming,' and a New York Times report from the epicenter — a remote gold-mining town — finds medical workers overwhelmed by deaths and infections. The U.S. State Department and CDC issued a coordinated response update May 29, committing rapid support. Uganda has also reported cases, raising cross-border containment concerns. Watch: whether WHO can secure sufficient ring-vaccination capacity in conflict-affected Ituri province. (BBC, May 30; NYT, May 30; AFP, May 30; State Dept., May 29)
2
Ethiopia's parliamentary and regional elections open Monday with roughly 50 million eligible voters, but Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed's Prosperity Party faces no credible challengers. BBC reporting notes that ongoing conflicts — particularly in Amhara and Oromia — have excluded significant portions of the population from voting. Campaign posters for Abiy line the streets of his rural hometown of Beshasha, Africanews reports. Opposition space remains constrained and results are expected to extend the ruling party's dominance. Watch: turnout figures in conflict-affected regions and international observer assessments. (BBC, May 31; Africanews, May 31)
3
Nigeria's 2027 presidential contest is crystallizing, with the Labour Party fracturing and two opposition candidates now declared. Africanews reports the NDC named former presidential candidate Peter Obi as its 2027 standard-bearer, setting up a three-way contest against President Bola Tinubu (APC) and Atiku Abubakar (ADC). Simultaneously, TheCable reports the Julius Abure-led Labour Party faction adopted national youth leader Kennedy Ahanotu as its presidential candidate, deepening the LP split. Watch: whether courts resolve LP's factional dispute before primary deadlines and whether Obi's NDC nomination consolidates the southern opposition vote. (Africanews, May 31; TheCable, May 31)

US-AFRICA POLICY
Trump Administration Presses African Nations to Trade Minerals and Health Data for U.S. Aid
The Wall Street Journal reports the Trump administration is conditioning aid packages on African governments providing access to critical minerals and health data, drawing pushback from multiple nations. The policy extends the administration's extractive-conditionality approach — previously seen in AGOA's lapse — into humanitarian programming. African governments are reportedly resisting terms they regard as sovereignty-compromising. The standoff raises questions about U.S. development financing across the continent at a moment when Chinese and Gulf financing offer alternatives. (WSJ)

The crisis illustrates how Botswana's commodity dependence translates directly into household health outcomes.
