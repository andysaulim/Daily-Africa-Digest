Morning Memo
1
Burkina Faso's junta severs all diplomatic ties with France, escalating the Sahel's break from Paris. Ouagadougou formally cut relations with France on Jun 28, accusing Paris of undermining national interests, according to BBC and Reuters. France said it would consider countermeasures. The move completes a triangle of French diplomatic expulsions across the Alliance of Sahel States: Mali and Niger acted earlier. Paris now has no resident ambassador in any AES capital, removing the last formal bilateral leverage channel and accelerating the pivot toward Africa Corps and other non-Western security partners. Watch whether France recalls embassy staff and whether the AES formalises a collective posture.
2
Uganda's military chief orders NTV and Daily Monitor closed, deploying soldiers to both newsrooms. General Muhoozi Kainerugaba, President Museveni's son and army commander, ordered the shutdown of Nation Media Group's NTV television and Daily Monitor newspaper on Jun 28, with military personnel physically occupying their Kampala offices, per BBC and Al Jazeera. Muhoozi stated publicly he does not believe in a free press. NMG described its facilities as under 'military siege.' The action is the most direct press suppression episode Uganda has seen in years and signals Muhoozi's willingness to deploy hard power ahead of any succession transition. Watch for international donor responses and NMG's legal challenge.
3
DRC's Ebola outbreak is testing containment as volunteers conducting safe burials face community violence. Frontline workers carrying out safe burials — critical to halting transmission — have come under physical attack from terrified communities in at least one major city, the New York Times reported Jun 27, citing correspondent Declan Walsh dispatching from the scene. The NYT flagged that experts fear this could become the worst Ebola outbreak on record. Burial teams face dual risks: infection and community hostility. Aid access constraints directly threaten the containment perimeter. Watch WHO response posture and whether DRC requests additional international health personnel.

Safe burials are the single most effective transmission-control measure; disrupting them removes the containment mechanism.
